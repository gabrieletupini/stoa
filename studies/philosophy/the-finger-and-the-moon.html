Short essay · on the universal

Two religions,
one moon.

There is the religion that is taught, and there is the religion that is true, and most people spend a lifetime mistaking the first for the second. Holding the two apart is most of the work — and the moment you do, every tradition starts saying the same thing.

I.

The finger and the moon.

Every tradition has two layers. The first is what can be written down: the words, the rituals, the names of God, the metaphysics, the cosmology, the calendar of feasts. This is the human work — the scaffolding built across centuries to point at something. The second is what the scaffolding points at: the thing itself, the reality that does not depend on which book you opened or which century you were born in.

The old Zen teachers had the cleanest image for the distinction. The finger points at the moon. The finger is not the moon. Do not mistake one for the other. Every collapse of religion into something violent or small starts from this single confusion.

The moon

What the finger points at

The reality every tradition reports the same when it gets quiet enough to listen. Has no doctrine because it precedes language. Cannot be lost, defended, or improved.

  • The peace that passes understanding
  • The disappearance of the line between self and world
  • What the Tao calls the unnameable
  • The shanti of the Bhagavad Gita
  • The "I am" before the next word
The finger

The pointing scaffold

The human artifact built to point at the moon. Necessary, useful, beautiful — and prone to being mistaken for the thing it points at. Every doctrine and ritual lives here.

  • Doctrines, dogmas, creeds
  • Names of God, ritual calendars
  • Lineages and orthodoxies
  • Buildings, books, schools
  • The vocabulary you grew up inside
II.

The moon does not need defending.

What every tradition reports, when it gets quiet enough to listen — the Christian mystic, the Sufi, the Buddhist sitter, the Hindu sage, the Hasid, the Quaker — is the same set of things, in close to the same order. A stillness underneath the surface noise. The collapse of the line between you and the rest of the world. The sense that everything is happening to and as one thing. The peace that, the New Testament says, passes understanding; that the Bhagavad Gita calls shanti; that the Tao Te Ching points at when it says the way that can be named is not the eternal way.

You can read all four side by side and the reports converge. Not because the authors copied each other — most never met — but because they were describing the same country from different doors. That country is the moon. It does not need a defender. It is already what is. You will not strengthen it by arguing for it. You will not weaken it by doubting it. It survives all conversation about it.

III.

The finger gets attached to.

The pointing finger is different. The finger is a human artifact, and humans get attached to their artifacts. The particular shape of the finger — the doctrine, the language, the lineage — becomes, in enough generations, the thing itself. The map becomes the country. The story about God becomes God. And then the question shifts from have I seen what this points at? to is my finger the right finger?

This is where things get expensive. And once they get expensive, they get loud. And once they get loud enough, they march.

IV.

The collective finger.

Consider what happens when a finger grows large enough to belong to a country. The same God of Abraham is invoked, today, by three traditions that have spent fourteen centuries killing each other over which prophet, which book, which uncle, which hill.

Sunni and Shia split in the year 632 over who should succeed Muhammad — not over the moon, over the finger — and the descendants of that argument are still detonating in Damascus and Baghdad. The Crusaders rode into Jerusalem in 1099 with a cross on their shields, killed the Jewish and Muslim inhabitants in their houses, and prayed in the same Holy Sepulchre at the end of the day. The Buddhist monks of Myanmar, whose central instruction is non-harm, have led mobs against the Rohingya. The Thirty Years' War — Catholics and Protestants pointing fingers at the same Christ — emptied a third of central Europe by 1648. The same human hand, holding similar fingers, pointing at slightly different angles, has caused more bloodshed than most plagues.

None of those wars were fought over the moon. They were fought over the angle of the finger. Mine is straighter. Mine has the right joint. Mine is in the right book. The collective ego attaches to the finger the way the personal ego attaches to a face in the mirror — it confuses the thing it identifies with for what it actually is. And once a million people have made the same confusion together, the finger has its own momentum, and it can move armies.

V.

The same machine, in a normal life.

You do not need to be running an empire to feel the cost. The same mechanism is at work in the friend who left the conversation hurt because you do not believe the same thing they do, in the family thread that goes quiet at Christmas because someone said one wrong sentence about faith, in the inner pressure to defend a position you have not actually examined since you were a child. Two believers of the same religion will argue about a footnote. Two within the same denomination will argue about a verse. Two within the same household will argue about how the verse should be read aloud. The argument is never about the moon. The moon is fine.

If you can hold the two layers apart in your own mind — the finger here, the moon there — you become harder to convert and easier to learn from. You can read the Sermon on the Mount and the Tao Te Ching and the Dhammapada in the same week and notice they are describing the same room from three sides. You can sit with someone of a different tradition and find more in common than not. You stop defending a finger. You also become less defensive about your own; the particular tradition you were raised in, or chose, is not less true for being one of many — it is one specific door into the same room. Loving your door does not require demolishing the others.

The moon does not need a defender. It is already what is. You will not strengthen it by arguing for it. You will not weaken it by doubting it. It survives all conversation about it.
VI.

What the Buddha actually said.

In the Kalama Sutta, when the people of Kalama ask the Buddha which of the many wandering teachers passing through their village to believe, his answer is famously the opposite of the answer of almost every other religious teacher in history. He does not tell them to believe him. He tells them not to.

"Do not go by reports, by legends, by tradition, by scripture, by logical conjecture, by inference, by analogies, by agreement through pondering views, by probability, or by the thought 'this contemplative is our teacher.' When you yourselves know — these qualities, when adopted and carried out, lead to welfare and to happiness — then enter and remain in them." — Anguttara Nikaya 3.65 (Kalama Sutta)

The line is usually paraphrased: do not believe anything I say if it does not resonate with you. The paraphrase is a little softer than the original, but the meaning survives. The Buddha is pointing at his own finger and telling you not to mistake it for the moon. Look at what I am pointing at. If you see it, you do not need me. If you do not see it, no amount of believing me will help.

Two and a half thousand years later, the instruction still costs nothing to follow. The moon is right where he said it would be. The finger is whatever finger you came in with — the one your grandmother handed you, the one you found in a book, the one you stopped trusting at fifteen and never replaced. The work is not to defend it. The work is to look up.

Two layers, in every tradition: the pointing finger and the moon it points at.
The moon: the same in every report, never needing defence.
The finger: a human artifact, defendable, attachable, mistakable for the thing itself.
The collective finger: moves armies. None of them are fighting over the moon.
The Buddha's instruction: do not believe me. Look at what I'm pointing at.
The work: not to defend a finger. To look up.
