Short essay · on anger

Anger is a fire
you light in your own house.

Anger feels like power aimed outward — at the boss, the headline, the body that won't behave. It is almost always the opposite: combustion turned inward, paid for by the one who lit it. Four things worth knowing before the next time the match strikes.

I.

Venting is the igniter, not the extinguisher.

The folk theory is hydraulic: anger is pressure in a boiler, and if you don't "let it out" it will burst. Freud half-believed it; Konrad Lorenz built a whole model of aggression on it. It feels true. It is, as far as the evidence goes, wrong.

When Brad Bushman put it to the test, people who vented — hitting a punching bag while picturing the person who had angered them — went on to behave more aggressively afterward, not less. The ones who simply sat with it, or were distracted, cooled down faster. (Bushman, 2002, Pers. Soc. Psychol. Bull.)

The reason is plain once you see it. Expressing rage is not draining a tank; it is rehearsal. Every shout, every replayed grievance, every imagined comeback runs the circuit again and lays the wire down thicker. Catharsis doesn't discharge the anger. It practices it. You don't empty the boiler — you widen the pipe so it fills faster next time.

II.

Underneath the anger is a demand for control you don't have.

Look closely at the moment of fury and you'll find a collision: what is, slamming into what you had decided ought to be. Your boss changed his mind. The news did what the news does. The symptom appeared. None of these asked your permission — and the anger is precisely the refusal to accept that they didn't have to.

Epictetus drew the only line that matters: some things are up to us — our judgments, our chosen response — and most things are not — other people, outcomes, the past, the next hour. Anger is the attempt to force the second category to obey the rules of the first. It is a grab at a steering wheel that was never connected to the wheels.

Notice the proportions: the heat of the anger is the exact size of the gap between your demand and reality. Shrink the demand — "I would have preferred it; I did not require it" — and the fire has nothing left to burn. (Epictetus, Enchiridion 1 & 5; Marcus Aurelius, Meditations.)

III.

It is poison you drink yourself.

The old line — passed around under the Buddha's name and a dozen others — is more literal than it sounds: holding onto anger is like grasping a hot coal to throw at someone else; you are the one who gets burned. Or, in the blunter version, resentment is drinking poison and waiting for the other person to die.

The person who triggered you may have forgotten by lunch. They may never know. But your heart rate knew, your blood pressure knew, your sleep that night will know, and your gut will know by morning. You measured out the dose and you swallowed it. The target is almost never in the room.

IV.

The body keeps the clock — and rumination resets it.

A trigger fires two systems on two timescales. The fast one — the sympathetic-adrenal axis — dumps adrenaline in seconds: the heat, the pounding chest, the narrowed vision. Once the threat passes, it clears within a few minutes.

The slow one is the HPA axis — hypothalamus to pituitary to adrenal gland — and it releases cortisol. Cortisol climbs over minutes and typically peaks around 15–30 minutes after the trigger, then takes roughly 60–90 minutes to drift back toward baseline, even when the event itself lasted ten seconds. (Dickerson & Kemeny, 2004, meta-analysis of acute stressors; Sapolsky, Why Zebras Don't Get Ulcers.)

The trigger ends in seconds. The cortisol curve runs for the better part of an hour on its own. But each time you replay the scene, you press the button again — the dashed line is a two-second event stretched into an afternoon. V.

So, the practice.

One

Name it as data

Anger is information: a demand of yours just met a reality that didn't comply. Read the message — then don't let it generalise into a verdict about the world.

Two

Don't vent — interrupt

Expression rehearses; it doesn't release. Change the channel: move the body, change the room, label the feeling. Distraction beats catharsis, every trial.

Three

Find the line of control

Ask of the thing: is any of this actually up to me? Act there, exactly there — and let the rest be what it already is, with or without your permission.

Four

Wait out the clock

Don't decide or speak at the peak. Give it the twenty minutes the chemistry needs to crest and start falling. The same words land differently on the way down.

Venting: feeds the fire.
Anger: a grab for control you were never given.
Holding it: poison you drink yourself.
The body: carries it for an hour — longer if you keep relighting it.
The only move that helps: notice, interrupt, locate control, and wait.
